ABOUT ME


Hi there! My name is Xihe Liu(刘羲和). I am an undergraduate student in the Physics Department at Shandong University, class of 2021. I am currently working in Dr.Tie Liu's ISM & Star formation group at Shanghai Astronomical Observatory, Chinese Academy of Sciences.

I'm interested in studying ISM and Star formation (particularly within the Central Molecular Zone) using continuum and molecular lines. I mainly analyze the data from the (sub)millimeter observations with interferometers (e.g., SMA and ALMA Band3 & Band6). Besides, I am also interested in using numerical simulation to study the star formation.

During my spare time, I enjoy indulging in life's pleasures, such as playing saxophone, reading, watching movies, and traveling.

Analysis of the CMZ Cloud 'e' using ALMA Band 3&6

The Central Molecular Zone (CMZ) presents an opportune locale for investigating star formation owing to its high temperature, high density, and strong turbulence in the gas. Nevertheless, the star formation rate within the CMZ is unexpectedly ten times lower than anticipated (Longmore et al. 2013a; Kruijssen et al. 2014; Barnes et al. 2017). This unanticipated attribute, coupled with the dearth of associated research investigations, has rendered the exploration of the CMZ region a prominent subject in recent years.

In preceding studies, a distinct area referred to as "Cloud 'e'" within the region of the CMZ was identified as harboring numerous dense cores. Consequently, this investigation employed higher resolution observations (0.3''*0.2'') obtained from ALMA bands 3 & 6 to corroborate these cores.

As shown in the figure on the right, we identify 33 dense cores simultaneously in both two bands (indicated by purple contours) using a signal-to-noise ratio of 5. Additionally, 9 cores are only detected in band3 (indicated by red contours), and 75 cores are exclusively detected in band6 (indicated by cyan contours).


In addition, as shown in the left figure, we also obtain the flux at each pixel of this region and calculate the spectral index at each pixel. We find that most of the spectral indices are greater than 2, indicating a good fit to optically thin dust emission.

We also calculate the spectral index and mass of each dense core and conduct a detailed analysis. We also analyze the fragmentation of filaments, spatial distribution of cores, and mass segregation in Cloud 'e'.
